Why Is Earth Harmed?

Greed and Abuse of Power

Powerful people, groups, and businesses do everything they can to retain their might, even if their actions are not in the best interest of the people. Politicians may push dubious narratives to retain voters, and corporations sponsor questionable research to decrease the credibility of those opposed to their interests. Furthermore, as shown above, the fallout from environmental destruction is uneqally distributed, most potent in countries that contribute to it the least as well as marginalized, minority-dominated communities within wealthy nations. This selfishness and pseudo-autocracy greatly undermines efforts to stop damage to the planet.

Other instances of abuse of power degrading the environment include but are not limited to:

Food Industry

As the globe became more densely populated and connected, food supply chains industrialized and spread globally. Producers and suppliers, as businesses, seek to maximize sales and speed and minimize costs. But with convenience came severe environmental damage. Factory farming, excessive packaging, and high levels of waste all contribute to the food industry's steep environmental footprint. The worst foods include:

Food Purposes Environmental Harms
Palm oil Semi-solid; cheap Deforestation; biodiversity loss
Meat and dairy Quality protein; consumed for millennia Methane emissions; deforestation for agriculture
Fast food/junk food Tasty; convenient; lucrative Not filling (increasing overall food demand); deforestation (ingredients); packaging materials
Military-Industrial Complex

War is extremely harmful to the planet, both due to the fighting itself and motives behind the fighting. Militaries account for 6% of all global CO2 emissions, and governments' desire for crude oil has exacerbated up to half of all interstate conflicts since 1973. The environmental impacts of war closely mirror those of general corporate greed and include:

Profits for defense contractors

Opportunities for oil/gas corps.

Habitat destruction

Wasted/damaged resources

Sources: Green Peace

Inertia

Society had long been dependent on unsustainable practices, perhaps since the Industrial Revolution, before environmental dangers became widely known. Phasing out these practices would benefit society in the long run but would be challenging and costly in the short term.

Unsustainable Yet Nutritious

Industrially-produced foods are terrible for Mother Nature but convenient for humans. Some planet-harming foods, like beef and eggs, also benefit human health. Alternatives may be more sustainable but nutritionally incomplete:

  • Meat has key nutrients like iron and Vitamin B12.
  • Alternative proteins like nuts also have key nutrients, like fiber and magnesium.
  • But the protein itself is superior in meat and dairy.
Fossil Fuels

Fossil fuels, like coal, oil, and gas, have long been known to be environmentally destructive. However, much of the world remains dependent on them. Phasing out these fuels would require massive changes, like restructuring power grids, that would be very difficult to execute. Renewable sources themselves also carry cons of their own:

Energy Source Potential Downsides
Nuclear Catastrophic impact of accidents
Wind Reliability; risk to birds
Hydro Water footprint; habitat destruction
Solar Reliability; land usage

Source: Green Match

Consumer Behavior

Frontend businesses, like grocery stores and restaurants, actually bear more responsibility here than consumers. The section title "Consumer Behavior" simply distinguishes these customer-facing establishments from large, backend corporations like the industrial producers who aim to maximize yields and savings. But consumers still indirectly enable unsustainable business practices.

Excess Waste

Consumerism and commercialization are two major facets of modern capitalism. You've seen it, even without realizing it: huge holiday decorations, entire aisles of Halloween candy, and mile-long lines at the drive-thru. The excessive selling and spending is certainly not a problem in and of itself; the issue lies with the waste generated from all that consumerism. Americans generate 25% more daily trash between Christmas and New Year's than at other times of the year. Consumerism also drives waste of the following items:

Plastic bags

Restaurant food

Packaging materials

Single-use items

Fad items like clothing

Source: Capital Hire

Collective Action Problem

A harsh reality is that one single person can do little for the planet. Modifying unsustainable routines burdens all individuals who do so, but the population will only benefit if everyone makes those sacrifices. To mitigate this issue, governments and businesses must focus on its root causes, which include:

Polarization

Lack of market regulation

Individualism

Misinformation

Industry-funded research

Many of these issues relate to greed, inequality, and imbalance of power. In fact, many of the obstacles to meaningful collective action are intertwined and entrenched in the vast, hyper-capitalist systems and institutions that define the current world order.

Overpopulation

A cold, hard truth is that there are simply too many of us. Each human requires food, water, and shelter, which all consume Earth's natural resources. The world's population surpassed eight billion in November 2022 and will continue rising. As the planet becomes more crowded, systemic change will only become even more difficult.

Road Expansion

Population growth and modernization in the mid-20th century has prompted construction of major highway systems globally. Today, with even higher numbers of both humans and cars, nations are building new roads in an attempt to mitigate ever-increasing congestion. This vicious cycle is very problematic and contributes to the following:

Roadways Vehicles
Habitat fragmentation CO2 emissions (largest sector)
Soil contamination Air pollution
Soil erosion Industrial waste
